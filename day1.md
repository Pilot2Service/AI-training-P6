## Session 1 — The New AI Mindset

> **The core idea of this session:** AI is not just a tool your company uses. It is a new way to build a company.

### AI is more than a productivity tool

Most people first meet AI as a faster way to do existing work: writing emails quicker, summarising documents, drafting copy. That is real, but it is the smallest part of the story. If you stop there, you have only made your old company slightly faster.

The bigger shift is this:

- AI is **not a tool your company uses.** It is closer to the system your company runs on.
- AI is **not only a productivity and efficiency booster.**
- AI is a **new capability** — it lets your company do things it simply could not do before.
- AI is **new capacity** — work that used to require hiring more people can now happen without growing headcount.
- AI **makes things faster.**
- AI **makes possible new products and solutions** that were previously too slow or too expensive to build.
- AI **enables new kinds of companies, new business models, and new competitive dynamics.**

The practical question for you is not "where can AI save us time?" It is: **what can you now do that was previously impossible, too slow, or too expensive?** And: how can a lean team build a product with massive reach?

> **The field is moving fast — and that is fine.** Do not wait for it to settle. Pick up the tools and build something. But do not get stuck at the tool level, because AI is much more than a set of tools: it is a paradigm shift for the whole business.

### The 3X and the 30X effect

Two different things happen when AI enters a team:

- **The 3X effect:** AI raises everyone's baseline. An average performer becomes noticeably more capable across writing, analysis, research, and building.
- **The 30X effect:** AI multiplies what your best people can do. A strong founder or operator who knows how to direct AI well does not get a little better — their output multiplies many times over.


The lesson: AI does not just lift the floor, it raises the ceiling dramatically for people who learn to work with it deliberately. The teams that win are the ones whose best people learn to direct AI, not just consume it.

### AI as the operating system your company runs on

Think of AI not as an app you open, but as an **intelligent layer** sitting underneath your whole business.

> **What "operating system" means here:** on a computer, the operating system is the layer everything else runs on top of. The image here is the same — every workflow, every decision, every process flows through an intelligent layer that is constantly learning and improving, rather than AI being one isolated app you visit occasionally.

In that picture, AI is not bolted on at the edges. It is in the middle, and the rest of the company is organised around it.

### Open loops vs. closed loops

To make this concrete, it helps to think about your business as a set of **loops**.

**An open loop** runs in one direction and then stops:

```
Input  →  Execution  →  Output
```

> **What an open loop is:** a controlled process with **no feedback.** Someone makes a decision, work gets done, an outcome appears — and that is it. There may be some measurement afterwards, but it often does not feed back into changing how the process runs next time.

Most companies, and most parts of companies, run as open loops. The problem is that open loops constantly lose learning. Every cycle, knowledge about what worked and what didn't leaks away instead of improving the next cycle.

**A closed loop** feeds its results back into itself:

```
Input  →  Execution  →  Output  →  Feedback  →  Adjustment  →  (back to Input) ...
```

> **What a closed loop is:** a process that is **self-regulating.** It watches its own outputs and adjusts how it works in order to keep meeting a goal. Closed loops are extremely powerful because they improve themselves over time.

Here is the key reframe for your company:

> **Your company is not one closed loop. It should be a set of closed loops** — many small, self-improving processes, each with a clear goal, run by agents and orchestrated together.

### Agents and orchestration

A couple of terms you will hear all day:

> **AI agent** = an AI system that can take a goal, decide on the steps to reach it, use tools, and act — not just answer a single question. Where a chatbot responds to one prompt, an agent can carry out a small process on its own: for example, monitor incoming orders, check them against rules, and handle the routine ones.

> **Orchestration** = coordinating several agents and tools so they work together toward a larger outcome, with each one handling the part it is good at.

> **Tool / agent registry** = a maintained list of the tools and agents available in your company, what each one does, and how to call it — so that work can be routed to the right place automatically.

A simple way to imagine the destination:

> *What if 80% of your orders ran in a closed loop handled by agents, and only the 20% that genuinely need human judgement reached a person?*

That is the kind of leverage we are looking for — not "AI helps us process orders faster," but "AI runs the routine and frees humans for the exceptions."

### Where does the human sit? Three positions

As you put AI into a loop, you choose how involved a person stays:

- **Human-in-the-loop:** a person reviews or approves each step before it proceeds. Highest control, slowest, best for high-stakes or early-stage processes you don't trust yet.
- **Human-on-the-loop:** the process runs on its own, and a person supervises and can step in when needed. A good middle ground once a loop is reliable.
- **Human-outside-the-loop:** the process runs fully automatically with no person involved per case. Fastest and most scalable, appropriate only when the loop is trusted and the cost of a mistake is low.

> **A blunt but useful truth:** people, and especially people's decisions, are usually the bottleneck. The goal is not to remove human judgement from things that need it — it is to stop human judgement from being a bottleneck on things that don't.

### So what does this actually require?

Turning your business into a set of closed loops is not only a software task. A few practical foundations matter:

- **Everything must be machine-readable, and AI must have access to it.** An agent can only act on information it can reach. Minimise human-only channels: reduce emails and direct messages, and when people do meet, record the meeting with an AI note-taker so the content becomes usable.

> **AI note-taker** = a tool that joins a meeting (or processes a recording) and produces a written transcript and summary, turning a conversation that would otherwise be lost into machine-readable text.

- **Design the loops.** Plan and build small closed-loop processes deliberately, one at a time. Do not try to automate the whole company at once.
- **Give every loop a goal.** A closed loop only works if it knows what it is trying to achieve and can measure progress toward it.
- **Orchestrate.** As you build more loops, you need a way to coordinate them and a registry of the tools and agents available.

### Exercise — Where does AI create the biggest leverage in your case?

The aim of this exercise is to find places where AI (used in an **advanced or agentic** way — not just making an existing process a bit faster) could open up genuinely new business possibilities for your startup. The best outcome is that you spot a new feature, workflow, product, or even business model that you have not properly considered yet — and on Day 2 you begin to prototype it.

Run this in your AI thinking partner — the project that already knows your business.

**Step 1 — Find the opportunities.** Paste this prompt:

```
You are a startup strategist who understands AI deeply, including agentic and
closed-loop approaches, not just basic productivity use of AI tools.

Look at my business (use everything you know about it from this project).
Identify 5 areas where AI could create genuinely NEW business possibilities
for us — not just making an existing process faster.

For each area, I want advanced or agentic uses: closed-loop processes,

were previously too slow, too expensive, or impossible.

For each of the 5 areas, give me:
- A short name for the opportunity
- What it is, in 2–3 sentences
- Why it is newly possible because of AI
- Roughly what would have to be true for us to do it

Be specific to my business. Avoid generic "use AI to save time" or "automate customerflow with AI agent" suggestions.
```

**Step 2 — Pressure-test and rank.** Once you have the five, paste this to look harder at their value:

Write shortly your own comments and evaluation, and add the following promtp:
```
Take the 5 opportunities above. For each one, evaluate:

- The business potential: how big could this be for us, and why?
- The customer value: what real problem does it solve, and for whom?
- The feasibility for a small team using today's AI tools (low / medium / high)
- What would be the smallest first version we could prototype this week?

Then rank the 5 from most to least promising for us right now, and explain
the ranking in one sentence each. Recommend the single one you would prototype
```

## Session 2 — Planning in the AI Era



> **The core idea of this session:** building is now fast, which means going in the wrong direction is also fast. Planning is what keeps the speed pointed at the right thing. https://youtube.com/shorts/64fqM75OKBo?si=ugVrw-WAYkvDoyrJ

### We now have a shared language

For most of computing history, humans had to translate their intentions into a special language for the machine — code. That translation step is largely being solved.

> **Why this matters:** increasingly, you no longer need a special machine language between you and the computer. Plain human-readable text is enough, often written as Markdown (`.md`). For the first time, the language you plan and think in is also the language the machine builds from. That is a genuinely big change — and it is why learning to write clear documents is now a core building skill.

### The biggest mistake: jumping straight into execution

The most common mistake with AI is rushing into building before thinking. Because AI work is fast, a vague request produces a fast mess. The more time you spend planning, the faster AI can help you build the *right* thing.

> **Forget the "one magic prompt" idea.** "Generate a whole website with a single prompt" is not how good work happens. An agent needs a **work order**, not half a thought. If you hand it an unfinished idea, *it* will fill in the gaps — and you will not have decided how. You should decide what AI fills in and what it does not. Create a clear path, not a guessing game that lurches forward by trial and error.

Three reasons planning genuinely matters:

- **Think for yourself first.** The plan forces you to make the decisions that matter before you spend effort building.
- **Execution is fast, so mistakes are expensive.** A wrong direction gets built quickly and at scale before you notice.
- **You prevent waste.** Good planning avoids long error-and-fix cycles, dead code, and tangled, confusing results.

### The main workflow

Keep this loop in your head for everything you build:

```
Spec  →  Plan  →  Implement  →  Test
```

- Always split implementation into small pieces.
- Always test each piece.
- Release in complete chunks, not half-finished fragments.

> **What a spec is:** short for *specification* — a clear written description of what you want to build and why, before any building starts. **What a plan is:** the ordered set of steps for actually building it. We will spend most of this session getting the spec right, because everything downstream depends on it.

### Start from the customer, not the technology

A spec never starts from a technical description. Even when you are building a single feature, that feature needs a reason to exist: **which customer problem does it solve?**

So begin with the customer's situation and your product vision.

**Step 1 — Write your vision in plain words.** Often the best move is to open a blank document and simply start writing:

- What does the customer need? How does the service work, and what does it do, from the customer's point of view?
- What is the customer's problem, and how significant is it?
- How is the problem solved? How does the customer use your service?
- What are your values, your principles, and where do you see the focus?

Write freely. Do not worry if it is not yet logical or well-ordered. Stream of consciousness is fine — separate paragraphs, half-formed thoughts, side points that matter. You are getting your thinking out where an AI can work with it.

**Step 2 — Create a planning partner.** Set up a project in ChatGPT or Claude and turn the AI into a thinking partner. Ask it to analyse your vision and tell you what *you* still need to define.

> **A quick word on prompting:** even for simple analysis, you get far better results when you give the AI three things — a **role** ("act as an experienced product strategist"), **context** (your vision and business), and **constraints** (what you want back, and in what form). We go deeper on prompting on Day 2, but use this pattern from the start. (If you are familiar with skills, add or create needed skills)

A good opening prompt for your planning partner:

```
Act as an experienced product strategist working with an early-stage startup.

Below (and in this project) is my rough product vision and customer situation.
Read it and help me sharpen it.

1. Tell me what is still vague or undefined and needs a decision from me.
2. Ask me up to 7 clarifying questions, ordered by how much they matter.
3. Point out any assumptions I seem to be making that I should check.

Do not write the plan yet — first help me think.
```

You can pass your product vision and the customer's situation back and forth for several rounds. Each round it gets sharper.

#### Tools to use with your planning partner

These are well-known product techniques. Here is what each means and how to prompt for it:

- **Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)** — a clear description of the specific type of customer who has your problem most acutely and is the best fit to serve first.
  > *Prompt idea:* "Explore several customer profiles that have this problem, describe each one, and prioritise which we should serve first and why."

- **Jobs To Be Done (JTBD)** — the underlying "job" a customer is really trying to get done when they reach for a solution. It looks past the product at the customer's actual goal and situation.
  > *Prompt idea:* "Run a Jobs To Be Done exercise with me. Go deeper into the customer's behaviour and the situation in which the problem must be solved, and what they are truly trying to achieve."

- **Need themes** — short, crystallised labels for the needs underneath those jobs, both functional (what it does) and psychological (the deeper *why*).
  > *Prompt idea:* "Convert the Jobs To Be Done above into Need Themes — give 5 functional and 2 psychological themes. Return a table. Each theme should be a one- or two-word noun phrase that crystallises the essence of the need, e.g. *affordability*, *relevance*, *confidence*."

- **AI-advantage scoring** — going through those need themes and scoring where AI gives you the biggest advantage, so you focus on opportunities where AI is a real differentiator rather than a nice-to-have.
  > *Prompt idea:* "For each need theme, score from 1–5 how much advantage AI gives us in serving it, and explain the highest-scoring ones. Where is our AI advantage greatest?"

**Step 3 — Get data to support your thinking.** Use your AI tool's **deep research** mode to ground your plan in reality.

> **Deep research** = a mode in many AI tools where the AI searches many sources, reads them, and produces a structured report with references, rather than answering from memory alone. Use it to study your customer and problem more deeply, look at existing solutions and competitors, and pull in relevant market or research data.

**Step 4 — Create your PRD.** This is the central planning document.

> **What a PRD is:** a *Product Requirements Document* — a written description of what you are building, for whom, and why, including what it must do and what is out of scope. **Why it matters for AI:** a coding agent builds from your PRD the way a contractor builds from blueprints. A clear PRD produces a coherent product; a vague one produces a confused one. The PRD is how you turn your thinking into a work order the AI can act on.

What a good PRD contains:

- **The problem and the customer** — who this is for and what pain it solves.
- **The product vision** — what the experience is, in the customer's words.
- **Core features** — what the product must do, described as outcomes ("the user can…"), not as technical implementation.
- **Scope and non-goals** — and, just as importantly, **what you are deliberately *not* building** in this version.
- **Success criteria** — how you will know it works.

What a PRD does *not* need: technical architecture decisions, exact technologies, or implementation detail. Leave those to the build phase. A good PRD is clear about *what* and *why*, and leaves *how* open.

> **What separates a good PRD from a weak one:** a good PRD makes decisions. It is specific about the customer, ruthless about scope, and explicit about what is left out. A weak PRD is a wish-list that leaves the important choices for the AI to guess at.
>
> **A note on MVP scope:** your first version should be a *minimum viable product* — the smallest thing that delivers real value and lets you learn. A good MVP spec is the same PRD with scope cut hard: one customer, one core job, the fewest features that prove the idea.

**Step 4b — Create the other plans you need.** Alongside the PRD, a few supporting documents make AI building much smoother:

- **Brand style and personality** — your tone, your voice, what your product should feel like.
- **Design system** — your colours, fonts, and the basic building blocks (components) of your interface, so everything looks consistent.
- **Skills plan** — definitions of specific capabilities for your AI tools and the agents you build.

> **What "skills" are:** a skill is a reusable, packaged capability you give an AI agent — a defined task it knows how to do well, with the instructions and context it needs. Instead of describing the same task from scratch every time, you give the agent a skill. You can write your own, and you can also get ready-made ones — for example from skill libraries like skills.sh, from GitHub, or from the skill ecosystems and marketplaces of the tools you use.

**Step 5 — Build a production plan with AI.** With the spec and supporting documents in place, work out *how* you will build: in what order, what comes first, and what is left out for now. A common pattern is to set up the basic structure first — the scaffolding such as sign-in, databases, and the empty shell of the product — and then build feature by feature on top of it.

> **Scaffolding** = the basic skeleton of an app (accounts, sign-in, data storage, navigation) without real content yet. You put the frame up first, then fill it in piece by piece.

**Step 6 — Take your plans to the build agent.** Hand your PRD and plans to the AI tool that will build (for us, Lovable on Day 2). Confirm the production plan and the main structural choices with it before building, and turn on any services you will need as you go. We start here on Day 2.

#### Planning tools at a glance

- **Deep research mode** — for grounding your plan in real evidence.
- **Design tools** — for look and feel: Claude's design view, Google Stitch, Figma, and others.
- **Your AI thinking partner** (ChatGPT / Claude project) — for sharpening the spec across several rounds.
